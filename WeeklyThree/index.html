An Argument Against The Poor Image

I honestly felt like I was missing a bigger joke or something in this article… Towards the beginning of the piece, I could wrap my head around some of what the author was arguing. Comments like “a high- resolution image looks more brilliant and impressive, more mimetic and magic, more scary and seductive than a poor one” made total sense to me and I agreed with it. But slowly, the author started to lose me. When I read things like “Resolution was fetishized as if its lack amounted to castration of the author” I couldn’t help but think:

And I do understand the meta-ness and irony of that image given how low quality it is! But that truly has never (and still doesn’t) really strike me as a social valuation or a technological castration of the person who took and shared this image. To me, it’s just… a meme. The photo was probably taken in 2007 and put on Facebook by someone sharing pictures from their family vacation. From there, someone zoomed into the guy in the background and shared it on their Facebook page or with their friends. Because it was taken on a point and shoot and added on Facebook in a low resolution so that it could be easily downloaded and shared, it’s not very good quality. But that’s kinda where the story ends for me. True, I guess the lack of resolution in this image attests to its appropriation and displacement, but jumping from that to “the dematerialized art object turns out to be perfectly adapted to the semioticization of capital, and thus to the conceptual turn of capitalism.” I don’t know if that’s me not being bright enough to understand that claim or if it is, what I like to call, reading into it. But then, where would all these deep-fried political memes come from? Maybe she knows something I don’t.
